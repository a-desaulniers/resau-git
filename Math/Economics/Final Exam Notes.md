>[!example] ### **IENG2005 Engineering Economics Terminology Cheat Sheet**
>### **General Terms:**
>
>- [ ]  **Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA)**: A method to evaluate the feasibility of projects by comparing their costs against their expected benefits.
>
>- [ ]  **Capital Cost Allowance (CCA)**: A system used by the Canadian government to allow businesses to write off a portion of the cost of assets over time for tax purposes.
>
>- [ ]  **Depreciation**: The reduction in the value of an asset over time due to wear, tear, and obsolescence.
>
>- [ ]  **Inflation**: The rate at which the general level of prices for goods and services is rising, leading to a decrease in purchasing power.
>
>### **Cash Flow and Time Value of Money Terms:**
>
>- [ ] **Cash Flow (CF)**: The amount of money moving in and out of a project or investment, typically on an annual basis.
>- [ ] **Present Value (PV)**: The current value of a future cash flow, discounted at a certain rate. 
>- [ ] **Future Value (FV)**: The value of a current cash flow at a specific point in the future, adjusted for interest. 
>- [ ] **Annual Equivalent (AE)**: The constant annual amount that is equivalent to a series of uneven cash flows over time. Useful for comparing projects with different cash flow patterns. 
>- [ ]  **Nominal Interest Rate**: The interest rate before adjusting for inflation, also referred to as the stated rate.
>- [ ]  **Effective Interest Rate (EIR)**: The actual interest rate after compounding over a period, adjusted for frequency of compounding.  
>
>### **Bond Terms:**
>
>- [ ]  **Bond**: A debt security in which an issuer borrows funds from investors, agreeing to pay back the principal with interest at specified intervals.
>- [ ]  **Issuer**: The entity (government, corporation, etc.) that issues a bond to raise capital.
>- [ ]  **Coupon Rate**: The fixed annual interest rate paid by the issuer on the bond's face value, expressed as a percentage.
>- [ ]  **Face Value (Par Value)**: The nominal value of the bond, typically $1,000, which is paid back to the bondholder at maturity.
>- [ ]  **Maturity Date**: The date on which the bondâ€™s face value is repaid to the bondholder.
>- [ ]  **Coupon Payment**: The periodic interest payment made to bondholders, calculated by multiplying the coupon rate by the face value of the bond.
>- [ ]  **Market Price**: The current trading price of the bond in the market, which may differ from the face value due to changes in interest rates.
>- [ ] **Premium Bond**: A bond trading above its face value, typically because its coupon rate is higher than current market interest rates.
>- [ ]  **Discount Bond**: A bond trading below its face value, often because its coupon rate is lower than current market interest rates.
>- [ ]  **Yield to Maturity (YTM)**: The total return expected on a bond if it is held until maturity, accounting for both the coupon payments and any capital gain or loss.
>
>### **Project Evaluation and Decision Making Terms:**
>
>- [ ] **Net Present Value (NPV)**: The difference between the present value of cash inflows and outflows for a project, used to determine its profitability. 
>- [ ]  **Internal Rate of Return (IRR)**: The discount rate at which the NPV of a project becomes zero, representing the project's rate of return.
>- [ ]  **Payback Period**: The time it takes for an investment to recover its initial cost through net cash inflows.
>- [ ]  **Benefit-Cost Ratio (BCR)**: The ratio of the present value of benefits to the present value of costs.
>- [ ]  **Replacement Analysis**: The process of determining whether it is more cost-effective to repair or replace an existing asset.
>### **Risk and Sensitivity Analysis:**
>
>- [ ] **Risk**: The uncertainty surrounding future cash flows or the potential for deviation from expected outcomes in a project.
>- [ ]  **Sensitivity Analysis**: A technique used to understand how the variation in one or more independent variables impacts the dependent variable (e.g., NPV).
>- [ ]  **Scenario Analysis**: Analyzing multiple possible future scenarios (e.g., best-case, worst-case) to assess the potential impact on project outcomes.
>### **Depreciation and Taxation:**
>
>- [ ]  **Straight-Line Depreciation**: A method of depreciation where an assetâ€™s value is reduced evenly over its useful life. 
>- [ ]  **Declining Balance Depreciation**: A method where depreciation is calculated based on a fixed percentage of the assetâ€™s remaining book value.
>- [ ]  **Capital Cost Allowance (CCA)**: A Canadian tax system for depreciating assets over time, using specific rates for each class of asset.
>- [ ]  **Tax Shield**: The reduction in income taxes due to allowable deductions, such as depreciation.
>### **Other Key Terms:**
>
>- [ ] **Sunk Costs**: Costs already incurred and irrelevant to future decisions.
>- [ ] **Marginal Cost**: The cost of producing one additional unit of output.
>- [ ] **Fixed Costs**: Costs that do not change with production levels.
>- [ ]  **Variable Costs**: Costs that change with production levels.

### **CCA Depreciation Problem**

**Problem:**
A company purchases a piece of equipment for $100,000. The equipment belongs to Class 8, which has a Capital Cost Allowance (CCA) rate of 20%. What is the CCA deduction for the first year and the undepreciated capital cost (UCC) at the end of the first year? What about the second year?

### **Solution:**

1. **Determine the Capital Cost Allowance (CCA) for the First Year:**

   The formula to calculate the CCA for the first year only is:

   $$
   \text{CCA} = \text{UCC at the beginning of the year} \times \frac{1}{2}\text{CCA rate}
   $$
   For each following years, you can depreciate at the full CCA rate

   For the first year, the **UCC** is simply the purchase price of the equipment:

   $$
   \text{UCC}_0 = 100,000
   $$

   The **CCA rate** for Class 8 is 20%, or 0.20.

   Therefore, the CCA for the first year is:

   $$
   \text{CCA}_1 = 100,000 \times \frac{1}{2}0.20 = 10,000
   $$

2. **Calculate the Undepreciated Capital Cost (UCC) at the End of the First Year:**

   The **UCC** at the end of the year is calculated as:

   $$
   \text{UCC}_1 = \text{UCC}_0 - \text{CCA}_1
   $$

   $$
   \text{UCC}_1 = 100,000 - 10,000 = 90,000
   $$

### **Answer:**
- **CCA for the first year**: $10,000
- **CCA for the second year**: $18,000
- **Undepreciated Capital Cost (UCC) at the end of the first year**: $90,000
- **Undepreciated Capital Cost (UCC) at the end of the second year**: $72,000

### **Risk Analysis with Probabilities Problem**

**Problem:**
A company is evaluating two investment projects, Project A and Project B, each with different probabilities of success. The company is considering the expected net present value (NPV) of each project, where the probabilities of success and failure, along with associated cash flows, are as follows:

- **Project A:**
  - Probability of success: ($P(\text{Success}) = 0.7$)
  - Cash flow if successful: $20,000
  - Probability of failure: \($P(\text{Failure}) = 0.3$)
  - Cash flow if failed: $5,000

- **Project B:**
  - Probability of success: ($P(\text{Success}) = 0.5$)
  - Cash flow if successful: $25,000
  - Probability of failure: \($P(\text{Failure}) = 0.5$)
  - Cash flow if failed: $8,000

The discount rate is **8%**. Calculate the **Expected NPV** for both projects and compare them.

### **Step 1: Calculate the Expected Cash Flows (ECF)**

The **Expected Cash Flow** (ECF) for each project is calculated by multiplying the probability of each outcome by the corresponding cash flow, then summing the results.

$$
\text{ECF} = P(\text{Success}) \times \text{Cash Flow (Success)} + P(\text{Failure}) \times \text{Cash Flow (Failure)}
$$

#### **Project A:**

$$
\text{ECF}_A = 0.7 \times 20,000 + 0.3 \times 5,000 = 14,000 + 1,500 = 15,500
$$

#### **Project B:**

$$
\text{ECF}_B = 0.5 \times 25,000 + 0.5 \times 8,000 = 12,500 + 4,000 = 16,500
$$

### **Step 2: Calculate the NPV for Each Project**

The **NPV** is the present value of the expected cash flow. Using the formula for NPV:

$$
NPV = \frac{\text{ECF}}{(1 + i)^n}
$$

Where:
- ( $i = 0.08$) (8% discount rate)
- ($n = 1$) (1 year)

#### **Project A:**

$$
NPV_A = \frac{15,500}{(1 + 0.08)^1} = \frac{15,500}{1.08} \approx 14,351.85
$$

#### **Project B:**

$$
NPV_B = \frac{16,500}{(1 + 0.08)^1} = \frac{16,500}{1.08} \approx 15,277.78
$$

### **Step 3: Interpretation**

- **Project A** has an expected NPV of **$14,351.85**.
- **Project B** has an expected NPV of **$15,277.78**.

Although **Project A** has a *higher probability of success*, **Project B** has a higher expected NPV because its cash flows, even with equal probability of success and failure, are more significant.



### **Conclusion:**

Based on the expected NPVs, **Project B** is *probably* a better investment as it provides a higher expected return after factoring in the probabilities of success and failure.

1. **Defender**: The existing asset you currently own and operate.
2. **Challenger**: A potential replacement asset.

    - Initial purchase cost.

    - Useful life.
